# Grammar 

## quantifiers 

### all
* used with plural
* used alone before numbers (three or more), after a subject pronoun for emphasis or to mean _everything that_
* We don't use it alone to mean _everything_ or _everyone_
   
> ~~All need the sun to grow.~~  
> _All nine_ planets orbit the sun.  
> _We all/All of us_ learn the names of the planets at school.  

### the whole
used before a _singular countable noun_

> _The whole_ civilization was based on its worship of the sun.
   
### either / neither
used before a _singular noun_. __Either/neither of__ + _plural noun_ is normally followed by a _singular verb_
   
> People would _either_ stay out of the sun completely or use lead oxide or arsenic to achieve the desired effect.  
> _Neither of the two_ was very good for you

For negative sentences, use __either__ with negative verb or __neither__ with an affirmative verb
Use __either__ to agree with negative sentence or __neither__ to disagree with negative sentence
> -- I don't like this film  
>   -- I don't like it too  
>   -- me _either_ (means 'I also don't like it')  
>   -- _neither_ do I (means 'But I like it')

Use __neither ..., nor ...__ and __either ..., or ...__
   
### some
used in offers
> Do you want _some_ coffee?

### each
used when we choose between two
### every
used when we choose between three or more

###  combinations
_each_, _all_, _any_, _either_, _neither_, _whole_ + _of_ + _noun_ | _posessive noun_ | _the_ ...  
~~every + of~~

## continuous forms

We use continuous forms to talk about actions or situations (past, present or future) which are incomplete, temporary or in progress  

- to talk about actions/situations which continued up to, or stopped shortly before, the main past event
  
> The workplace had been struggling with a very negative work culture

- to talk about smth in progress around, or as background to, the main past event
  
> Two old people were laughing uncontrollably

- to talk about the length of time of an action/situation which started in the past and is still going on
  
> We've been watching them work all morning

- to talk about temporary actions that are in progress around now
  
> Everything is going on round you

- to talk about what will be in progress at a particular time in the future
  
> Tomorrow afternoon, we'll be visiting an amazing fish market

We do not usually use the continuous form when we talk about states with verbs like _like_, _know_, _seem_, etc
> I like your new office. ~~I'm liking your new office~~  
> I know a dozen ways to cook salmon. ~~I'm knowing a dozen ways...~~

## compound adjectives and nouns

### Compound adj
can be written with a _hyphen_, especially before a noun, and formed in various ways, including:
  
  - noun/adj/adv + present or past verb particle
  
> a _man-eating_ tiger, a _brightly-lit_ room
   
  - noun + adj
  
> a _world-famous_ actor
   
  - number + noun (note that noun does not take a plural form after a number)
  
> an _eight-hour_ day
   
### Compound nouns
can be written as one word, hyphenated or as separate words.  
The order is the key to meaning: e.g. _chocolate milk_ is a __type of milk__  
whereas _milk chocolate_ is a __type of chocolate__.  
Though other combinations are possible, two common ways to form compound nouns are:
  
  - noun + noun
  
> bus stop, football
   
  - adj + noun

> blackboard, software

## noun phrases

We can divide the noun phrase into three parts:

before noun | central noun | after noun
------------|--------------|-----------
_Another surprising_ | _fact_ | _about these insects_


__before__ the noun can go:  
    - determiners _the_, _each_, _several_, _such_  
    - adj _beautiful_, _fascinating_, _lovely_  
    - adv + adj _very intriguing_, _really dangerous_  
    - possessives _its_, _whose_, _the goverment's_  
    - nouns _Oyamel_, _compass_, _TV_  

__after__ the noun can go:  
    - prepositional phrases _on BBC2_, _for children_  
    - adv phrases _this evening_, _by car_  
    - clauses _(which are) found in nature_

## relative clause

_who_, _which_, _that_, _where_, _whom_

- identifying r.c. (defining)
- non-defining r.c. - can be omitted, separated by comma, can't include _that_

> The wealth^subj1 ~~which^obj~~ they^subj2 have accumulated

- _what_ - can be used __only__ in questions  
- _that_ - can be used with people  

### identifying r.c.
* directly follows the thing it is identifying  
  We use the relative pronouns _who_/_whom_ (for people), _which_ (for things), _that_ (for both) and _whose_ (for people).
>  The woman _who called earlier_ is here.  
>  Have you seen those documents _which were on my desk_?  
>  There's the guy _whose party we went to_.
* we can leave out the relation pronoun when it defines the object of the verb
>  Is that the shirt you bought yesterday? <-> Is that the shirt _that_ you bought yesterday?
* when a relative pronoun is followed by _be_, we can usually leave out _be_ and the relative pronoun. This is sometimes known as a _reduced relative clause_. This occurs with continuous tences, the passive and prepositional phrases and adjectives
>  They've found the money stolen in the robbery. <-> They've found the money _which was_ stolen in the robbery.
* _verb/adj/noun + preposition_ combinations usually stay together
>  What's that song you were _listening to_ a few minutes ago?
* we can use the relative adverbs _when_ and _where_
>  Christmas is time _when_ families get together.
we can leave out _when_ after some _time_ words and _where_ after _somewhere_, _everywhere_, _anywhere_ and _nowhere_
>  The 6th is the day we get back from our holiday. <-> The 6th iss the day _when_ we get back from our holiday.  
>  Do you know anywhere we can get a coffeee? <-> Do you know anywhere _where_ we can get a coffee?

### non-identifying r.c.
* can add information to a noun or can comment on the whole of a previous clause  
  it is separated with a comma from the thing it is adding information to.
>  He was born in Cowley, _which is a suburb of Oxford_.  
>  Redgrave, _whose political heyday was in the 1980s_, died in 2007.  
>  They went $2 million over budget, _which mean cuts had to be made elsewhere_.
* we use the relative pronouns, _which_, _who_ or _whose_. We don't use _that_ in a _non-identifying r.c._
>  ~~He was born in Cowley, that is a suburb of Oxford.~~
		 
## would

###  common usage

* express hypothesis
* make what we say more tentative or polite
* as the past form of _will_

To express smth hypothetical, often as part of a [conditional structure](#conditionals) or with _if only/wish_

> I'_d_ love to be a games developer  
> I'_d_ have told you if I'd known  
> I wish it _would_ stop raining  

In the phrases _would sooner_, _would rather_ and _would prefer_ when talking about preference

> I'_d_ rather go for a walk than sit in front of a computer screen for hours  

To make a polite offer or request. _would_ adds an element of tentativeness, tact or politeness

> What _would_ you like to drink - tea or coffee?  
> I'_d_ just like some water actually if that's OK  

To express or report a refusal in the past. _Would_ is used in this way as a past form of _will_

> No one _would_ help me  

In the phrases _would say_, _would imagine_, _would have thought_ with similar meaning to _think_ or to express speculation. _Would_ adds an element of tentativeness

> I _would_ say the new game is going to be a best-seller  
> It's going to be a long day, I'_d_ imagine  
> Where are Ali and Dina? I'_d_ have thought they'd be here by now  

To talk about future in the past. This is when you're talking about what actually happened from the perspective of an earlier time

> Super Mario, which first appeared in 1985, _would_ go on to become one of the most popular computer games of all time  
> At the time, no one realized he _would_ become such a well-respected politician  

To talk about repeated actions or habits in the past. THe meaning is similar to _used to_

> As a child, we _would_ visit my grandparents in Scotland every summer  

In speaking, we often pronounce _would_ as _'d_
  
> I think a taxi'_d_ be a better idea  
> No one'_d_ help me  
> I wish Peter'_d_ told me  

in writing, however, we generally only use _'d_ after pronouns    

__i.e.__

as past form of _will_
- to talk about an event that was in the feature at a past time
  
> I didn't realize it _would_ get so addictive

- to talk about an agreement or refusal in the past
  
> I asked some friends to try them out, but they _wouldn't_

with _would_, you can be inderect, less definite, more polite
- as part of expressions with _sooner_ or _rather_ to express a preference

> They _would sooner_ play shooting games and I think that would be true of lots of gamers

- as part of expressions with _say_, _imagine_, _have thought_
  
> I _would have thought_ that the games would turn out to be pretty dull

- to make a polite request or offer
  
> _Would_ you tell us what happened?

_would_ can describe how things were in the past
- to talk about repeated actions or habits in the past
  
> When I was at school, the teachers _would make_ us do these educational games

### conditionals

To talk about _hypotheses_, we use a __past tense_ in the main clause and we usually use _would_ or a __modal verb__ to talk about the hypothetical consequence or outcome.

For a hypothetical situation in the present or future, we use __past simple/continuous__ + _would/might/could/should_. This is a _second conditional_

> We _could go_ for a walk if it _wasn't raining_  
> If an election _was called_ tomorrow, how _would_ you _vote_?  

For a hypothetical situations in the past with a past consequence/outcome, we use __past perfect__ + _would/might/could/should have_. This is a _third conditional_

> What if we'_d set off_ earlier, _would_ we _have got_ there in time?  
> If you'_d been_ a bit quicker, you'_d_ probably _have got_ a ticket  

For a hypothetical situation in the past with a present consequences/outcome, we use __past perfect__ + _would/might/could/should_. This is a _mixed conditional_

> If we'_d surveyed_ more people, the results _would be_ a bit more reliable  

For a hypothetical situation in the present with a past consequence/outcome, we use __past simple/continuius__ + _would/might/could/should have_. This is a _mixed conditional_

> If I _didn't know_ him, I _wouldn't have said_ hello, would I?  

We can use _imagine, suppose, supposing, assuming_ as alternatives to _if_

> _Imagine_ how your life would be different if you'd been born a centry earlier  
> _Suppose_ you won the lottery, what's the very first thing you'd do?  
> _Assuming_ you could do anything, what job would you most like to have?  

We can also begin conditional structures with __were__ and __were ... to ...__ as an alternative to _if + past tense_ and __had__ as an alternative to _if + past perfect_. These forms tend to sound more formal

> _Were_ it not for Henri's help, we'd be in a complete mess now  
> _Were I to_ lose my job, I've no idea what I'd do  
> _Had you been_ a bit quicker, you'd probably have got a ticket  

__i.e.__

To talk about hypotheses we can use past tenses to
- describe an imaginary/hypothetical situation in the present or future (_conditional 2_)

> If you _were_ to interview the students, that _would_ lead to less accurate predictions  

- talk about a hypothetical (unreal) situation in the past with a present outcome     
- talk about a hypothetical (unreal) situation in the past with a past outcome (_conditional 3_)

> If I _had known_ then what I know now, I _wouldn't have made_ that decision  

- ask about a hypothetical situation in the past, present or future

> Supposing you _were asked_ to judge a meal in a restaurant, what do you think the answer _would_ depend on?  

### past tense and would for unreal situations

We often use past tenses to talk about improbable or unreal situations and often when we are exppressing smth we desire. We use the __past simple/continuous_ to express such situations in the present or future and we use the _past perfect__ for situations in the past.
  
We use _would rather/sooner_ + subj + _past tense_ to talk about what we prefer someone to do or what we want to happen. _Would sooner_ is perhaps more informal than _would rather_ and more used in speaking

> I'_d rather you didn't smoke_ in the apartment if you don't mind  
> We'_d sooner they spoke_ to us directly  

Note that when _would rather/sooner_ is __not__ followed by a subj, we use an infinitive w/o _to_ rather than a _past tense_

> I'_d rather/sooner commute_ than actually move and live in London  
> _Would_ you _rather go_ or stay a little longer?  

We can also sometimes use a present tense after _would rather/sooner_. This can make what we are saying seem more direct.

> I'_d rather_ you _don't smoke_ in the apartment if you don't mind  

We use _it's time_ + _past simple/continuous_ to talk about smth that has to be done now or in the very near feature. We can also use _it's high time_ and _it's about time_

> _It's about time_ we _were going_  

We can use _act/behave, etc_ + _as if/as though_ + _past tense_ to show that a present situation is untrue

> She's _behaving as if_ she _owned_ the place  

We use _wish/if only_ + _past tense_ to talk about wishes and regrets

> I _wish_ I _could_ speak better French  
> _If only_ I _had_ more money  
> _If only_ you'_d told_ me earlier  
> She _wishes_ she'_d said_ smth at the time  

We use _wish_ + _would_ to talk about what we want to change in the future

> I _wish_ Sara _would stop_ telling everyone what happened  

__i.e__

- _would rather/sooner_ + past simple to say that one person would prefer someone to do/not do smth

> Can I tell her about it?  
> I'_d rather_ you didn't  

> I'_d rather_ people didn't know about it  

- _it's high/about time_ + past simple to talk about smth that has to be done now or in the near feature

> _It's about time_ you learnt to take care of yourself  

- _act/seem/look, etc_ + _as if/as though_ + past simple/past perfect to show that smth is or was unreal

> He _acts as though_ he was the manager (but he isn't)  
> She _looked as if_ she had seen a ghost (but she didn't)  

- _I wish/if only_ + past simple/past perfect to talk about wishes and regrets

> _If only_ I felt more confident  
> _I wish_ I could speak in public w.o feeling so self-conscious  
> _I wish_ I hadn't sent that email  
> _If only_ I'd thought before I sent that email  

- _I wish/if only_ + _would_ to talk about what you want someone else to do

   __I wish__ people would listen to what I have to say
