## Stocks

### call option
**Trader** can have a deal with a **broker** that *trader* can buy  
stocks for fixed price within a month. *Trader* pays some extra money  
for such opportunity.

### gamma squeeze
When stocks price rises too high and [call options](#call option)  
become unprofitable for *brokers*, they can buy stocks for current  
price to minimize difference between increasing stocks price and  
*call option* price. Massive buying leads to further increasing of  
stocks price and **gamma squeeze** leads to [short squeeze](#short squeeze)  
and immense rise of stocks price.

### margin call
*Brokers* who are loaned from to secure their finances  
can require that all money from [short](#short) *step 2* are kept by themselves  
until the moment of returning the loan.  
They also require **M + x** money, where **x** is a safety deposit  
in case if the price of stocks will go up instead of down.  
If prices go up, *brokers* can ask for **margin call** to increase  
**x** and cover risks.

### short
Considering that stocks cost is expected to dip from **M** to **N**, **N<M**  
* loan stocks
* sell stocks for **M** price
* wait until loan deadline
* buy stocks for **N** price
* return the loan
* profit: **M - N - loan comissions**

### short squeeze
When there are a lot of [shorts](#short) and something else happens,  
e.g. political event, news, some unexpected facts, i.e. anything  
that leads to increase of stocks price.  
Massive [margin calls](#margin call) happen, that leads to closing  
a lot of *short positions*, because traders can't pay *margin call*.  
Then a lot of traders begin to try to buy stocks to close their *shorts*  
(i.e. to produce *steps 4-5*). But the amount of available stocks is  
lower than required, that leads to further increasing of stocks cost  
because sellers see the high demand. The result is the rise of stocks  
cost without control.
